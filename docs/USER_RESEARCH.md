# Bolvi Care — User Research

This is my synthesis of the discovery work I ran for Bolvi Care in and around Everett / South Snohomish County. I'm writing it down so anyone evaluating the company can see what I actually heard from caregivers, seniors, families, and a discharge planner — and, more importantly, which product and business decisions that evidence drove.

## 1. Method and sample

This synthesis compiles **47 structured data points** drawn from a broader set of 100+ discovery conversations. I gathered them through physical paper surveys, Google Forms, and in-depth interviews, all in the Everett / South Snohomish County market I'm building for.

The 47 break down as:

- **~38 supply-side** — existing and prospective Care Partners (caregivers).
- **~7 demand-side** — seniors, family coordinators, and one hospital discharge planner.
- **2 in-depth qualitative interviews** on top of the survey counts.

| Instrument | Respondents |
|------------|-------------|
| Physical surveys — existing caregivers | ~6 |
| Physical surveys — prospective caregivers | ~21 |
| Physical surveys — family coordinators | ~4 |
| Physical survey — discharge planner | 1 |
| Google Forms — caregiver experience | 1 |
| Google Forms — senior/client survey | 2 |
| Google Forms — prospective Care Partners | 10 |
| In-depth qualitative interviews | 2 |

The physical survey counts were transcribed from scanned paper, so exact figures carry some noise; the Google Forms responses are the cleanest quantitative signal and I lean on them where precision matters.

A note on timing: I ran this research in **March 2026, before several strategic decisions were finalized** (the W-2 employment model, the rate tiers, and the membership design). Where the early framing in my raw synthesis conflicts with the model Bolvi actually operates today, **this document reflects the decisions actually made.**

## 2. Segments

I found five distinct segments. For each I've noted the core motivation and the quantitative signals behind it.

### Segment 1 — Existing Care Partners (n≈7)
CNAs, home health aides, EMTs, and informal companions, mostly with 3–5+ years of experience.

- **Core motivation:** pay fairness and professional respect. They believe they receive only ~50–70% of what families actually pay, and that perceived gap drives resentment toward agencies.
- Current hourly pay clusters at **$20–28**; the minimum they'd accept for a 15+ hr/week commitment is **$30–50**.
- Top stated exit reasons: low pay (#1), then burnout / unpredictable hours, then a better opportunity.
- Strong preference for a **quality-gated** platform (one that rejects 30–40% of applicants).
- Worker classification splits: some want employee status for insurance and protections, others want contractor status for higher pay and autonomy.
- Multiple respondents confirmed they had continued working directly with clients after leaving an agency.

### Segment 2 — Prospective Care Partners (n≈31)
My largest and most strategically important supply segment — 21 paper + 10 Google Forms respondents, overwhelmingly full-time healthcare workers (hospital/clinic staff, EMTs, nursing students) with certifications ranging from CPR/BLS to CNA to EMT/Paramedic to LPN/RN.

- **Core motivation:** flexibility plus meaning — supplemental income that fits around an existing healthcare job and feels more personal than institutional work.
- In the cleanest sample (Google Forms, n=10): **10/10** work in healthcare, **10/10** have paid caregiving experience, **10/10** are willing to complete a background check, and **10/10** chose "flexible hours I can fit around my current schedule" as their top signup driver.
- Minimum hourly rates spread across $21–24 (3), $25–29 (3), $30–34 (1), $40–44 (2). Payment cadence preference: weekly (7 of 10).
- Nominal classification preference: **independent contractor (5), employee (2), whichever pays more (2)**.
- Top requests to join: **liability insurance at no cost (7 of 10 — the clear #1)**, an emergency button during visits (6 of 10), and guaranteed pay if a client cancels last-minute (5 of 10).
- What would make them confident starting: **clear written instructions per client (7 of 10)** and **paid training (6 of 10)**.
- Biggest concerns about entering homes: liability / legal risk (**7 of 10**), plus personal safety and fear of a false accusation.

### Segment 3 — Seniors / direct clients (n=2 + qualitative)
My most under-sampled but potentially most important demand segment. Both survey respondents live independently and neither currently receives help.

- **Core motivation:** independence and dignity — help on their own terms, not a feeling of dependence.
- Both rated the most important caregiver quality as "reliability" / "respects my independence," and both said having the **same person each time** is "Very Important."
- Both said they'd be "much more comfortable" if a caregiver already knew their routines, and both wanted to choose a caregiver from profiles/reviews.
- **Price sensitivity is existential:** on a **+$5/hr** increase, one would reduce hours and the other would **stop receiving care entirely**.
- They split on pre-visit information sharing (one comfortable with a call, one "not comfortable sharing"), which tells me intake can't be one-size-fits-all.

### Segment 4 — Family care coordinators (n≈4 + 2 qualitative)
Adult children and relatives managing care, ranging from same-home to different states.

- **Core motivation:** safety and peace of mind. This segment is fear-driven, not primarily price-driven — the nightmare is abuse, neglect, or incompetence they can't see.
- Monthly care spend ranges from under $1K to $4K+; they'll pay **$30–50/hr** for a vetted caregiver with visit tracking and a guaranteed replacement.
- Feature priorities ranked: background check (#1), a written update after every visit, a panic button, and a guaranteed replacement.
- Asked how they know what happens during a visit, the typical answer was "the caregiver tells me" or "I don't really know."
- A **24-hour replacement guarantee** and a **shared care timeline** for siblings both drew strong positive responses.
- The two qualitative interviews reinforced this. One coordinator described managing care across state lines for an older adult who had delayed seeking help to avoid burdening the family and then had an acute cardiac event; the recurring friction was transportation to appointments and the lack of short-notice respite for the primary family caregiver. A second, comparatively well-resourced coordinator was still overwhelmed by appointment logistics for a relative with a serious diagnosis; their core ask was modest — a trustworthy, experienced caregiver and a brief post-visit note that the person was safe — at a willingness to pay of roughly $26–40/hr.

### Segment 5 — Hospital discharge planner (n=1)
One discharge planner managing multiple patients per week who need non-medical home support.

- **Core motivation:** reliable same-day placement they can refer to without it falling through.
- They have only **1–2 reliable agencies** to refer to today. Placements fail because there are no same-day providers, agencies reject low-hour cases, families can't afford agency rates, and intake is slow.
- When a placement fails, patients stay in the hospital longer, go home unsupported, or return to the ED — they estimated **preventable readmissions a few times per month**.
- Same-day/next-day capability "would solve one of my biggest problems," and a post-visit update would be "very valuable — I never get that kind of follow-up."
- To earn referrals they require proof of vetting, liability insurance, and a track record of a few successful cases.

## 3. Confidence levels

I want to be explicit about how much weight each conclusion can bear, because the sampling is uneven.

- **Supply-side sampling is strong (n≈38).** The prospective and existing Care Partner findings — especially the Google Forms cohort — are consistent across instruments and large enough to act on.
- **Demand-side documentation is thin (n=7)**, and within it the most important voices are the smallest samples: **n=2 seniors** and **n=1 discharge planner**. I treat these as **directional, not established.** A single discharge planner interview is a strong signal about a channel, not proof of one; two senior responses point at a real pattern without sizing it.

I do **not** present the n=1 and n=2 findings with the same confidence as the n≈38 supply-side findings, and I've written Section 4 accordingly. **The clear next research priority is deeper demand-side work** — substantially more seniors, family coordinators, and discharge planners — before I'd treat any demand-side conclusion as settled.

## 4. What the research changed

This is the section that matters most: each finding below is tied to a decision it actually drove.

**Prospective Care Partners nominally preferred contractor status — but everything they asked for is a W-2 benefit.** Their stated classification preference leaned contractor (5 of 10). Yet their #1 requested reason to join was **free liability insurance (7 of 10)**, and their other top asks — **guaranteed cancellation pay (5 of 10)** and **paid training (6 of 10)** — are things only an employer provides. The employment model answers their stated *need* even where it contradicts their stated *preference*, so Bolvi employs Care Partners as W-2 staff rather than optimizing for the preference they'd name on a survey.

**Self-pay seniors would cut or quit care over $5/hr — so I created a distinct rate tier instead of an across-the-board discount.** Because a +$5/hr increase pushes this group to reduce or stop care entirely, discounting all customers to protect them would be indiscriminate and margin-destroying. Instead Bolvi carries a **distinct $39/hr self-pay senior rate**, separate from the $45/hr family-managed standard rate, so affordability is targeted at the population that actually needs it.

**Families ranked guaranteed replacement and post-visit visibility highest — so those became the membership, not a price cut.** The features families valued most weren't cheaper hours; they were a **24-hour replacement guarantee** and structured visibility into each visit. Those became the core benefits of the **$35/mo Premium Family Membership**, which deliberately carries **no hourly discount** — an hourly discount would have been margin-negative at typical usage, and it isn't what families said they wanted anyway.

**Discharge planners need same-day placement and proof of a track record — so that channel is sequenced for Year 2.** The planner needs same-day capability *and* requires proof of vetting, insurance, and a track record before referring. Since the track record has to exist first, Bolvi builds **Year 1 direct-to-consumer** to earn exactly that record, and sequences the **discharge-planner channel for Year 2** once there are successful cases to point to.

**Care Partners want 5–20 hr/week micro-commitments around existing healthcare jobs — so the staffing model is flexible part-time by design.** The supply is already employed in healthcare and wants supplemental hours on its own schedule ("depends on schedule" was the most common availability answer). Bolvi's staffing model is built around **flexible part-time scheduling** rather than full-time roles, because that matches the workforce that actually exists here.

## 5. Open questions

Things I still consider unvalidated and want to resolve before over-committing:

- **Demand-side sizing.** The senior (n=2) and discharge-planner (n=1) findings are directional. I need materially larger samples before treating any of them as established — this is the top priority.
- **Does the stated contractor preference actually flip?** My read is that Care Partners will prefer W-2 once the benefits are concrete, but I haven't tested that against people who've experienced both under Bolvi.
- **Self-pay senior conversion.** A $39/hr tier addresses the price wall in principle; whether self-pay seniors actually convert and retain at that price is unproven.
- **Membership willingness-to-pay.** Families responded well to the *benefits*; the $35/mo price point itself needs validation at scale.
- **Discharge-planner channel viability.** One enthusiastic planner is a hypothesis about a channel, not a proven one — it depends on Bolvi first building the track record they require.
- **Pricing generally.** Sample sizes here are sufficient for direction and hypothesis generation, not for statistical significance. I'd expand the sample before finalizing pricing.
